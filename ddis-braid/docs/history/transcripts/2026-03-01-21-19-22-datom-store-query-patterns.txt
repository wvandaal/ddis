*themselves* entities in the store. This means:\n\n1. **They have transactions** \u2014 you can time-travel to see what the uncertainty of entity X was at any point in history.\n2. **They have provenance** \u2014 you can see which agent computed a given authority score and when.\n3. **They can be contested** \u2014 if two agents compute different authority scores for the same (agent, entity) pair, that's a conflict on `:authority/score`, which is itself subject to resolution. The system can reason about disagreements about authority.\n4. **They contribute to their own inputs** \u2014 an uncertainty measurement is a fact in the store, which means it contributes to the commitment weight of the entity it measures. Measuring uncertainty *stabilizes* the entity (increases its forward causal cone). This is the right behavior: the act of assessment itself adds information.\n\nPoint 4 is a fixed-point property. The uncertainty measurement is a function of the store state, and it's also a fact *in* the store. This creates a potential circularity: measuring uncertainty changes the store, which changes the uncertainty. But because the store is append-only and measurement only adds facts (never removes them), and because the commitment function w(d) is monotonically non-decreasing, this converges. Each measurement can only *decrease* uncertainty (by adding information) or leave it unchanged. It cannot increase it. This is the same contraction property that guarantees DDIS's bilateral loop converges.\n\n```\nINV-MEASURE-001: Measurement Contraction\nComputing and asserting an uncertainty measurement for entity e \nMUST NOT increase the scalar uncertainty of e.\n\nFormally: let S' = S \u222a {measurement datoms for e}.\nThen uncertainty(e, S') \u2264 uncertainty(e, S).\n\nThis holds because:\n  - \u03c3_e (epistemic) can only decrease: we've added observed attributes\n  - \u03c3_a (aleatory) is unchanged: measurement doesn't assert conflicting values\n  - \u03c3_c (consequential) may increase slightly (new dependents) but is \n    bounded by the monotonic growth of w(e)\n\nThe net effect is non-increasing because the epistemic reduction \ndominates the consequential growth for well-weighted \u03b1, \u03b2, \u03b3.\n\nViolation: An uncertainty measurement computation triggers a \ncascade of recomputations that increases the measured entity's \nuncertainty above its pre-measurement level.\n\nVerification: Assert pre-measurement and post-measurement uncertainty \nscalars; verify post \u2264 pre.\n```\n\nActually \u2014 I need to pause here and be honest. The claim that measurement is always contractive is *plausible* but not *proven*. The consequential uncertainty term \u03c3_c depends on the uncertainty of dependents, and adding a measurement fact creates a new dependent. In pathological cases with tight feedback loops, this could oscillate. The correct statement is:\n\n```\nINV-MEASURE-001 (REVISED): Measurement Contraction (Conditional)\nComputing and asserting an uncertainty measurement for entity e \nMUST NOT increase the scalar uncertainty of e, PROVIDED that the \nmeasurement entity is excluded from the consequential uncertainty \ncomputation of its own target.\n\nFormally: \u03c3_c(e) is computed over dependents(e) \\ {measurements of e}.\n\nThis exclusion prevents the self-referential feedback loop while \npreserving the measurement's contribution to commitment weight.\n```\n\nThis is the kind of subtlety that the ChatGPT conversation would have glossed over with \"it's monotonic.\" The exclusion clause is small but critical \u2014 without it, the fixed-point property doesn't hold.\n\n---\n\n## Layer 5: This is the expected behavior.\n\n### Combined Uncertainty Scalar\n\n```rust\nfn scalar_uncertainty(\n    store: &Store, \n    entity: EntityId,\n    cache: &mut HashMap<EntityId, f64>,\n) -> f64 {\n    let sigma_e = epistemic_uncertainty(entity, store);\n    let sigma_a = aleatory_uncertainty(entity, store);\n    let sigma_c = consequential_uncertainty(entity, store, cache);\n    \n    // Weighted L2 norm\n    // \u03b1, \u03b2, \u03b3 are system parameters stored as datoms in the meta-schema\n    let alpha = store.system_param(\":uncertainty/alpha\"); // default 0.4\n    let beta  = store.system_param(\":uncertainty/beta\");  // default 0.4\n    let gamma = store.system_param(\":uncertainty/gamma\"); // default 0.2\n    \n    (alpha * sigma_e.powi(2) \n     + beta * sigma_a.powi(2) \n     + gamma * sigma_c.powi(2))\n    .sqrt()\n}\n```\n\nThe default weights emphasize epistemic and aleatory uncertainty equally (0.4 each) with consequential uncertainty weighted lower (0.2). Rationale: epistemic and aleatory uncertainty are *actionable* \u2014 you can reduce them by exploring more or deliberating more. Consequential uncertainty is *structural* \u2014 it depends on the graph topology, which changes slowly. Overweighting it would cause the system to be overly cautious about well-understood entities that happen to be heavily depended upon.\n\nThese weights are themselves facts in the store and can be adjusted per deployment.\n\n---\n\n## Stratum 3: Authority Computation\n\nThe spectral authority derivation requires building the agent-entity contribution graph and computing its decomposition. This is not expressible in Datalog \u2014 it's linear algebra. But the *inputs* are Datalog queries, and the *outputs* are datoms in the store.\n\n### Building the Contribution Graph\n\n```datalog\n;; QUERY: contribution-edges\n;; All (agent, entity, weight) triples where the agent has contributed\n;; to the entity through verified assertions.\n\n[:find ?agent ?entity (sum ?weight)\n :where\n [?datom _ _ ?tx :assert]\n [?tx :tx/agent ?agent]\n [?datom :entity/type _]              ;; ?datom is about an entity\n \n ;; Weight = 1 for unverified, 2 for witnessed, 3 for challenged-confirmed\n ;; Computed via a helper rule:\n (contribution-weight ?datom ?weight)]\n\n;; RULE: contribution-weight\n;; Weight of a contribution based on its verification status\n\n(contribution-weight ?datom 3) :-\n  [?datom _ _ ?tx],\n  [?witness :witness/invariant ?inv],\n  [?challenge :challenge/witness ?witness],\n  [?challenge :challenge/verdict :confirmed],\n  ;; the datom is about an entity maintained by this invariant\n  [?inv :inv/owner-module ?mod],\n  [?datom :entity/type ?type].         ;; simplified; real version checks \n                                        ;; module-entity relationship\n\n(contribution-weight ?datom 2) :-\n  [?datom _ _ ?tx],\n  [?witness :witness/invariant ?inv],\n  [?witness :witness/status :valid],\n  (not [?challenge :challenge/witness ?witness]\n       [?challenge :challenge/verdict :confirmed]).\n\n(contribution-weight ?datom 1) :-\n  [?datom _ _ ?tx],\n  (not [?witness :witness/invariant _]  ;; no witness covers this\n       (contribution-weight ?datom 2)),\n  (not (contribution-weight ?datom 3)).\n```\n\n### Spectral Decomposition\n\n```rust\n/// Build the bipartite adjacency matrix and compute its SVD.\n/// Returns authority scores per (agent, entity) pair.\nfn compute_spectral_authority(store: &Store) -> Vec<AuthorityScore> {\n    // Step 1: Get contribution edges from Datalog\n    let edges: Vec<(AgentId, EntityId, f64)> = store.query(\n        \"[:find ?agent ?entity ?weight\n          :where (contribution-edge ?agent ?entity ?weight)]\"\n    );\n    \n    // Step 2: Build bipartite adjacency matrix\n    // Rows = agents, Columns = entities, Values = contribution weights\n    let (agent_idx, entity_idx, matrix) = build_adjacency_matrix(&edges);\n    \n    // Step 3: SVD decomposition\n    // A \u2248 U_k \u00d7 \u03a3_k \u00d7 V_k^T\n    // k = min(50, agent_count, entity_count)\n    let k = 50.min(agent_idx.len()).min(entity_idx.len());\n    let svd = truncated_svd(&matrix, k);\n    \n    // Step 4: Authority scores\n    // For agent i on entity j:\n    //   authority(i,j) = \u03a3_d (U[i,d] \u00d7 \u03a3[d] \u00d7 V[j,d])\n    // This is the reconstruction of the original matrix through the \n    // low-rank approximation \u2014 it captures the \"structural\" contribution\n    // pattern, not just the raw edge weight.\n    let mut scores = Vec::new();\n    for (agent, i) in &agent_idx {\n        for (entity, j) in &entity_idx {\n            let score: f64 = (0..k)\n                .map(|d| svd.u[(*i, d)] * svd.sigma[d] * svd.vt[(d, *j)])\n                .sum();\n            \n            if score > 0.0 {\n                scores.push(AuthorityScore {\n                    agent: *agent,\n                    entity: *entity,\n                    score,\n                    contribution_count: direct_contribution_count(\n                        &edges, *agent, *entity\n                    ),\n                });\n            }\n        }\n    }\n    \n    scores\n}\n```\n\n### Why Spectral and Not Just Raw Contribution Count\n\nThe spectral decomposition captures something that raw contribution counting misses: **transitive authority.** If agent \u03b1 has contributed heavily to entities A, B, and C, and entities A, B, and C are all closely related to entity D (through cross-references or shared attributes), then agent \u03b1 has authority over entity D *even if it has never directly touched D*. The SVD captures this by projecting agents and entities into a shared latent space where proximity reflects structural similarity.\n\nThis is the same mathematical machinery DDIS already uses for LSI search (SVD over the term-document matrix). Applied to the agent-entity matrix instead, it gives you authority instead of relevance. The analogy is precise: LSI finds \"this document is relevant to this query because it's similar to other documents that match\" \u2014 spectral authority finds \"this agent has authority on this entity because it has contributed to structurally similar entities.\"\n\n### Delegation Threshold Computation\n\n```datalog\n;; QUERY: delegation-threshold\n;; The minimum authority required to work on a given entity.\n;; Derived from graph metrics and consequential uncertainty.\n\n[:find ?entity ?threshold\n :in $ ?entity\n :where\n [?metrics :metrics/entity ?entity]\n [?metrics :metrics/betweenness ?betweenness]\n [?metrics :metrics/in-degree ?in-degree]\n [?unc :uncertainty/entity ?entity]\n [?unc :uncertainty/consequential ?sigma-c]\n \n ;; Schema cardinality factor: entities with more cardinality-one\n ;; attributes are more conflict-prone\n [(entity-conflict-surface ?entity) ?surface]\n \n ;; Threshold formula:\n ;; threshold = normalize(\n ;;   w1 * betweenness + w2 * in-degree + w3 * \u03c3_c + w4 * surface\n ;; )\n [(* 0.3 ?betweenness) ?t1]\n [(* 0.2 ?in-degree) ?t2]\n [(* 0.3 ?sigma-c) ?t3]\n [(* 0.2 ?surface) ?t4]\n [(+ ?t1 ?t2 ?t3 ?t4) ?threshold]]\n\n;; RULE: entity-conflict-surface\n;; Fraction of an entity's attributes that are cardinality-one\n;; (and therefore susceptible to conflict)\n\n[(entity-conflict-surface ?entity) ?surface] :-\n  [?entity _ _ ?tx],\n  [(count-attrs ?entity :one) ?one-count],\n  [(count-attrs ?entity :all) ?all-count],\n  [(/ ?one-count ?all-count) ?surface].\n```\n\n### The Delegation Decision\n\n```datalog\n;; QUERY: can-resolve\n;; Can agent ?agent resolve conflicts on entity ?entity?\n\n[:find ?agent ?entity\n :in $ ?agent ?entity\n :where\n [?auth :authority/agent ?agent]\n [?auth :authority/entity ?entity]\n [?auth :authority/score ?score]\n \n [?deleg :delegation/entity ?entity]\n [?deleg :delegation/threshold ?threshold]\n \n [(>= ?score ?threshold)]]\n\n;; QUERY: delegation-decision\n;; What is the delegation status of entity ?entity?\n\n[:find ?entity ?decision\n :in $ ?entity\n :where\n [?unc :uncertainty/entity ?entity]\n [?unc :uncertainty/scalar ?u-scalar]\n \n ;; Count agents that can resolve\n [(resolver-count ?entity) ?rc]\n \n ;; Decision logic:\n ;; rc > 0 AND u_scalar < 0.2  \u2192 :delegatable\n ;; rc > 0 AND u_scalar >= 0.2 \u2192 :contested  \n ;; rc = 0 AND u_scalar < 0.5  \u2192 :escalated\n ;; rc = 0 AND u_scalar >= 0.5 \u2192 :human-required\n [(delegation-classify ?rc ?u-scalar) ?decision]]\n```\n\nThe threshold values (0.2, 0.5) are configurable system parameters stored as datoms.\n\n---\n\n## Stratum 4: Conflict Detection and Routing\n\n### Structural Conflict Detection\n\n```datalog\n;; QUERY: detect-conflicts\n;; Find all entity-attribute pairs with concurrent conflicting values.\n;; This is the core conflict detection query.\n\n;; Two datoms conflict iff:\n;; 1. Same entity and attribute\n;; 2. Different values\n;; 3. Both currently live (in LIVE index)\n;; 4. Attribute has cardinality :one\n;; 5. Neither tx causally precedes the other (concurrent)\n\n[:find ?entity ?attr ?val-a ?tx-a ?val-b ?tx-b\n :where\n [?entity ?attr ?val-a ?tx-a :assert]\n [?entity ?attr ?val-b ?tx-b :assert]\n [(!= ?val-a ?val-b)]\n [(!= ?tx-a ?tx-b)]\n \n ;; Cardinality check\n [?attr :db/cardinality :db.cardinality/one]\n \n ;; Concurrency check: neither is ancestor of the other\n (not (causal-ancestor ?tx-a ?tx-b))\n (not (causal-ancestor ?tx-b ?tx-a))\n \n ;; Both are still live (not retracted)\n ;; (Handled by querying against LIVE index)\n ]\n\n;; MONOTONICITY: This query contains negation (not causal-ancestor).\n;; However, causal-ancestor is MONOTONICALLY GROWING \u2014 adding new \n;; causal links can only ADD ancestry paths, which means the negation \n;; can only REMOVE pairs from the conflict set (a previously-concurrent \n;; pair might become causally ordered after learning about intermediate \n;; transactions). This means the conflict set is MONOTONICALLY \n;; NON-INCREASING with respect to causal information.\n;;\n;; In practice: an agent might detect a \"false conflict\" because it \n;; hasn't yet learned about the causal path between two transactions. \n;; After merging, the false conflict disappears. This is the \n;; conservative failure mode: over-detection of conflicts, never \n;; under-detection.\n```\n\nThis monotonicity property is important enough to state as an invariant:\n\n```\nINV-CONFLICT-CONSERVATIVE-001: Conservative Conflict Detection\nThe set of detected conflicts at any local frontier MUST be a \nsuperset of the conflicts that would be detected at the global \nfrontier (union of all agents' knowledge).\n\nFormally: conflicts(frontier_local) \u2287 conflicts(frontier_global)\n\nThis holds because learning about new causal paths can only \nresolve apparent concurrency (turning concurrent pairs into \ncausally-ordered pairs), never create new concurrency.\n\nConsequence: An agent may waste effort resolving a conflict that \ndoesn't actually exist (another agent's assertion was causally \nprior but the causal link hasn't propagated yet). This is safe \u2014 \nthe resolution produces a redundant datom that is harmlessly \ndeduplicated on merge. The system never MISSES a real conflict.\n```\n\n### Conflict Severity and Routing\n\n```rust\n/// Route a detected conflict to the appropriate resolution mechanism.\nfn route_conflict(\n    store: &Store,\n    entity: EntityId,\n    attr: AttrId,\n    datom_a: DatomId,\n    datom_b: DatomId,\n) -> ResolutionRoute {\n    // Step 1: Check if the attribute has a lattice\n    if let Some(lattice) = store.lattice_for(attr) {\n        let val_a = store.value_of(datom_a);\n        let val_b = store.value_of(datom_b);\n        \n        if let Some(joined) = lattice.join(val_a, val_b) {\n            // Lattice resolves it deterministically\n            return ResolutionRoute::Automatic {\n                method: ResolutionMethod::LatticeJoin,\n                winner: joined,\n                confidence: 1.0,\n            };\n        }\n        // Values are incomparable in the lattice \u2014 fall through\n    }\n    \n    // Step 2: Check if LWW resolves it\n    let hlc_a = store.hlc_of(datom_a);\n    let hlc_b = store.hlc_of(datom_b);\n    if hlc_a != hlc_b {\n        let winner = if hlc_a > hlc_b { datom_a } else { datom_b };\n        // LWW resolves it, but with lower confidence\n        return ResolutionRoute::Automatic {\n            method: ResolutionMethod::LWW,\n            winner: store.value_of(winner),\n            confidence: 0.7, // LWW is deterministic but arbitrary\n        };\n    }\n    \n    // Step 3: Compute severity for escalation\n    let w_a = store.commitment_weight(datom_a);\n    let w_b = store.commitment_weight(datom_b);\n    let severity = w_a.max(w_b) as f64;\n    \n    // Step 4: Find agents with authority to resolve\n    let resolvers: Vec<AgentId> = store.query(\n        \"[:find ?agent\n          :in $ ?entity\n          :where (can-resolve ?agent ?entity)]\",\n        entity\n    );\n    \n    if resolvers.is_empty() {\n        return ResolutionRoute::Human {\n            severity,\n            reason: \"No agent has sufficient authority\".into(),\n        };\n    }\n    \n    // Step 5: Route based on severity thresholds\n    let threshold_auto = store.system_param(\":conflict/threshold-auto\");\n    let threshold_agent = store.system_param(\":conflict/threshold-agent\");\n    \n    if severity < threshold_auto {\n        // Low severity: best-authority agent resolves automatically\n        let best = resolvers.into_iter()\n            .max_by_key(|a| store.authority(*a, entity))\n            .unwrap();\n        ResolutionRoute::AgentAutomatic {\n            agent: best,\n            severity,\n        }\n    } else if severity < threshold_agent {\n        // Medium severity: agent resolves with human notification\n        let best = resolvers.into_iter()\n            .max_by_key(|a| store.authority(*a, entity))\n            .unwrap();\n        ResolutionRoute::AgentWithNotification {\n            agent: best,\n            severity,\n            notify: store.human_agent(),\n        }\n    } else {\n        // High severity: human decides\n        ResolutionRoute::Human {\n            severity,\n            reason: format!(\n                \"Severity {} exceeds agent threshold {}\", \n                severity, threshold_agent\n            ),\n        }\n    }\n}\n```\n\n---\n\n## Stratum 5: DDIS Bilateral Loop Queries\n\nThese implement the specific DDIS operations over the The uncertainty signal gets weight 0.12, reflecting its importance as a coordination-layer metric. The other weights are slightly reduced proportionally.\n\n### Crystallization Readiness\n\nThis is the query that answers \"is this discovery finding ready to become a spec element?\"\n\n```datalog\n;; QUERY: crystallization-candidates\n;; Findings whose stability exceeds the crystallization threshold\n;; AND whose parent thread has sufficient confidence.\n\n[:find ?finding ?thread ?stability\n :where\n [?finding :finding/status :refined]           ;; must be refined, not raw\n [?finding :finding/thread ?thread]\n [?thread :thread/status :active]\n \n ;; Stability check\n [?stab :stability/entity ?finding]\n [?stab :stability/score ?stability]\n [(>= ?stability 0.7)]                         ;; configurable threshold\n \n ;; Confidence check on parent thread\n [?thread :thread/confidence ?conf]\n [?conf :confidence/depth ?depth]\n [?conf :confidence/coherence ?coherence]\n [(>= ?depth 0.6)]\n [(>= ?coherence 0.6)]\n \n ;; No unresolved conflicts on this finding\n (not [?conflict :conflict/entity ?finding]\n      [?conflict :conflict/status ?cs]\n      [(!= ?cs :resolved)])]\n```\n\nThis query has a `not` clause (no unresolved conflicts), making it non-monotonic.
